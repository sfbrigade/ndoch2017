The National Day of Civic Hacking is an international event for people to focus on improving their community. Thousands of people from around the world will tackle civic-oriented projects around open data, disaster relief, mapping, safety and justice, economic development, and other topics.

Here in San Francisco, we are producing a jam-packed weekend focusing on how to stay resilient in the face of change. We encourage attendees to engage in making our city more resilient. There will be workshops on user research, human-centered design, technical spec'ing, and other activities designed to help a project be impactful.

San Francisco faces risks on many fronts. Resiliency is measured in how well our institutions, communities, and infrastructure can weather hardships and even disasters. Earthquakes, sea level rise, droughts, blackouts, fires, floods, economic stress, and the housing crisis are just a few challenges that have tested our resilience in recent decades and can test us in the future. What fresh ideas and solutions can we develop this weekend through a human centered design process?

We invite you to join us: participate in the weekend, sponsor the event, or partner with us.

A Resilient City + National Day of Civic Hacking

In 1915, San Francisco put on a show for the whole world. The Panama-Pacific International Exposition showcased the leading innovations of the time from art to technology. More than 18 million people showed up to experience this world's fair. Also on showcase, just 9 years after the devastating 1906 earthquake, was San Francisco's remarkable recovery.

This is an historic reminder of San Francisco's resilience in the face of disaster. But throughout its history, the city by the bay has shown resilience in the face of all sorts of change, both human-made and natural.

For this year's National Day of Civic Hacking, Code for San Francisco invites your participation in building a more resilient city. We believe a resilient city is one that is connected and prepared for uncertainty. The two day event will:

  • Showcase work being done to make San Francisco more resilient
  • Encourage projects addressing challenges of resiliency through workshops in user research, human and community-centered design, technical spec'ing and other activities
  • Foster collaboration around resiliency by bringing urbanists, civic hackers, developers, designers, marketers, students, government staff, and passionate residents together to connect, learn, and build tools that help the city in the face of change

While we don't expect 18 million people this year, in the spirit of the exposition over 100 years ago, let's show the world that this is a resilient city.
